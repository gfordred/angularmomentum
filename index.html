# Angular Momentum
Interactive Educational Module

Angular Momentum

From intuition to rigorous rotational dynamics

p = mv → L = r × p = Iω

🌀 What is it?

Angular momentum is the rotational analogue of linear momentum. Just as a moving object resists changes to its straight-line motion, a spinning object resists changes to its rotation.

It captures how much rotational motion an object possesses, with respect to a chosen reference point.

🏒 The Spinning Skater

A skater spinning with arms outstretched has a certain angular momentum L. When she pulls her arms in, her moment of inertia I decreases.

Since L = Iω is conserved (no external torque), her angular velocity ω must increase. She spins faster.

🪐 Planetary Orbits

Planets sweep out equal areas in equal times (Kepler's 2nd Law) because angular momentum is conserved. When a planet approaches the sun, radius decreases → speed increases to keep L constant.

Gravity acts toward the centre → zero torque about the sun → L conserved.

The Mathematics

Linear momentum: p = mv
Particle angular momentum: L = r × p = r × mv
Magnitude: |L| = rp sin θ = rmv sin θ
Rigid body (fixed axis): L = Iω
Torque: τ = dL/dt
Units: kg·m²·s⁻¹

Common Misconceptions

✗ Myth: Angular momentum is just "spinning fast". It depends on both speed AND the distribution of mass (moment of inertia).
✗ Myth: L is absolute. Angular momentum depends on the chosen reference point / axis.
✗ Myth: Radial motion contributes. If v is parallel to r (sin θ = 0), angular momentum is zero.
✗ Myth: Energy and angular momentum are both conserved together. Energy can change while L is conserved (skater example).

The Right-Hand Rule

The direction of L = r × p is given by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand from r toward p, and your thumb points in the direction of L.

For counterclockwise rotation (viewed from above), L points upward (+z). For clockwise, it points downward (−z).

Key insight: Angular momentum is a pseudovector — its direction tells you the axis of rotation AND the sense of rotation by the right-hand rule.

Why does kinetic energy change?

The skater does internal work by contracting their muscles to pull arms inward. This work adds to the kinetic energy. Angular momentum is conserved because there is no external torque — but energy can still change through internal forces doing work.

K = L²/(2I)

Since L is constant but I decreases, K must increase. The extra energy comes from the skater's muscles.

Kepler's 2nd Law: Equal areas in equal times. dA/dt = L/(2m) = constant when L is conserved. The planet moves faster when closer (smaller r, larger v to keep L = mvr constant).
Why zero torque from gravity?

Torque τ = r × F. Gravity acts along r (toward the centre), so r × F = rF sin 0° = 0. The central force produces no torque about the origin, so dL/dt = τ = 0, meaning L is conserved.

Full Derivation
τ = dL/dt
Integrating: L(t) = L₀ + τ·t
Since L = Iω: ω(t) = ω₀ + (τ/I)·t = ω₀ + α·t
θ(t) = θ₀ + ω₀t + ½αt²

These are the rotational kinematic equations — exact analogues of the linear ones.
